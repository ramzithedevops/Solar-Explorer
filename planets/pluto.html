Pluto, once considered the ninth planet, is now classified as a dwarf planet. It lies in the Kuiper Belt and is known for its elongated, eccentric orbit. Discovered in 1930, Pluto has a thin atmosphere and five known moons, the largest being Charon. Its surface is a mix of rock and ice, with some regions featuring mountains as high as 3,500 meters (11,000 feet). Despite its reclassification in 2006, Pluto remains a subject of fascination and continues to be studied by space missions like New Horizons.

Interesting Facts, Myths, and Scientific Discoveries

  • Pluto's orbit is so elliptical that it sometimes comes closer to the Sun than Neptune.
  • Pluto has a heart-shaped region known as Tombaugh Regio, discovered by the New Horizons mission.
  • Charon, Pluto's largest moon, is almost half the size of Pluto, making them a binary system.
  • Pluto has an atmosphere that expands when it is closest to the Sun and contracts when it moves away.
  • Despite its small size, Pluto has a complex geological history with evidence of glaciers and potential subsurface oceans.
